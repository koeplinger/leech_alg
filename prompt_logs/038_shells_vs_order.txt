Prompt 038 — Shells vs. order: what closure means
===================================================
Date: 2026-04-12

User prompt (verbatim):
-----------------------
The roots of L have norm 2, so when you say it closes under octonion
multiplication, does that mean that a product made from octonions
assigned to each of the roots would multiply to an lattice point with
norm 4? The similar question is then for the inner shell of the Leech
lattice, where all lattice points have norm sqrt(8), does that mean
that the product of any two points there would multiply to a lattice
point with norm 8? How can you say that such a product forms a
(maximal) order? I see you wrote "due to bilinearity" which implies
scaling by an integer. That sounds right on the surface, however, I
would be comfortable to accept it if a lattice product actually closes
on the roots (or inner shell). But if it doesn't close, then how can
one conclude on this being an order?

Assistant response (summary):
-----------------------------
Confirmed the user's observation and explained what "order" means:

1. E₈ case: products of roots (squared norm 2) land at squared norm 4,
   because the octonion product is norm-multiplicative. The root system
   is NOT closed — it cannot be, by norm-multiplicativity alone. What
   is closed is the full lattice L = D₈⁺, which contains all shells.

2. Leech case: the twisted product ⋆ is NOT norm-multiplicative (only
   ~47% of pairs satisfy N(u⋆v) = N(u)N(v), per Section 5). The
   empirical norm distribution on Min(Λ) × Min(Λ) is
   {16, 32, 48, 64, 80, 96, 112, 128} — NONE of these is 8. The
   minimal shell is not closed; products land on higher shells of Λ.

3. Nevertheless, (Λ, +, ⋆) is an order in the ring-theoretic sense:
   Λ is a free Z-module of rank 24, contains a Q-basis of R²⁴, and
   (by Theorem 1.1) Λ ⋆ Λ ⊆ Λ. Shell-closure is NOT part of the
   definition of an order.

4. The bilinearity argument is rigorous, not hand-wavy. Explained
   concretely for L: we take a Z-basis {b₁,...,b₈} of L (8 vectors
   that span L over Z, not 8 roots); verify all 64 products b_i · b_j
   are integer combinations of the basis; then for any a,b ∈ L,
   write a = Σ α_i b_i, b = Σ β_j b_j with α,β ∈ Z, and
   a·b = Σ α_i β_j (b_i · b_j) is a Z-linear combination of elements
   of L, hence in L. The same argument applies to Λ with Min(Λ)
   generating Λ over Z.

Proposed paper update (accepted by user in prompt 039):
- Add a remark near Corollary 1.2 (or in Section 2.3) explicitly
  stating that Λ ⋆ Λ ⊆ Λ means products land somewhere in Λ
  (generally on higher shells), not in the minimal shell — and that
  this is consistent with an order in the ring-theoretic sense.
