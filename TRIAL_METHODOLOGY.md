# Trial Methodology

This document defines the methodology for all trial files (`trial_*.py`) in this
project. Each trial tests whether a proposed 24-dimensional Z-module (algebra
over the integers) closes under multiplication on the Leech lattice Λ.

---

## Goal

Find a highly symmetric rank-24 Z-algebra whose multiplication is closed on the
Leech lattice Λ. Because lattices are closed under addition by definition, it is
sufficient to test whether the proposed multiplication rule is closed.

Specifically, we test closure on the **minimal shell** of Λ: the 196,560 vectors
of squared norm 8 (in the standard R²⁴ metric). These are the shortest nonzero
vectors in Λ and play the structural role that roots play in root lattices.

---

## Philosophy

The first trials are expected to **fail**. This is intentional. The goal is not
to guess blindly ("try this!", "try that!") but to establish a controlled
methodology:

1. **Understand the anatomy of failure.** When a trial fails, we must understand
   *why* it failed — which products leave the lattice, by how much, in what
   pattern. This understanding guides the design of subsequent trials.

2. **Move toward a controlled goal.** Each trial should be informed by the
   failures of previous trials. We refine the multiplication rule based on
   structural understanding, not random search.

3. **Accept negative results.** It is possible that no such algebra exists.
   People have searched for a long time. What matters is understanding *exactly*
   when that conclusion is reached and *why*.

## Trial file structure

Each trial is a standalone Python file named `trial_NNN_short_description.py`
located in `python_project/src/`. Each file must contain:

### 1. Header documentation
- A docstring explaining the algebra being tested.
- The mathematical definition of the multiplication rule.
- References to any prior work or theory motivating the construction.

### 2. Algebra definition
- Explicit construction of the 24-dimensional multiplication (structure
  constants), typically by defining how 24 basis vectors multiply.
- Clear mapping between basis indices and the constituent algebras.

### 3. Sanity checks
Before testing against the Leech lattice, verify:
- **Norm check**: Compute the squared norms of products of minimal-shell vectors.
  If all products have norm > 8 (i.e., they overshoot the minimal shell), apply
  a global scaling factor so that at least some products land at norm 8.
- **Integrality check**: Verify that products of integer/half-integer vectors
  remain integer or half-integer (as appropriate for the Leech lattice embedding).
- **Symmetry check**: Verify that the multiplication has the symmetries claimed
  in the docstring.

### 4. Closure test
- Take a representative sample (or all) of the 196,560 minimal-shell vectors.
- For each pair (or a well-chosen subset), compute the product.
- Check whether the product lies in Λ.
- If the product is nonzero and has squared norm 8, check whether it lies on the
  minimal shell.

### 5. Failure analysis
When a trial fails (product leaves Λ), report:
- **How many** products fail out of how many tested.
- **Failure modes**: Which Wilson condition(s) are violated? What norms do the
  failing products have? Are the failures concentrated in specific vector
  families (type 1, 2, or 3)?
- **Near misses**: Are any failing products "close" to the lattice? What is the
  distribution of distances from Λ?
- **Pattern recognition**: Is there a structural pattern in the failures that
  suggests a modification to the multiplication rule?

### 6. Summary
A concise verdict: what was tested, what passed, what failed, and what was
learned. This summary should be actionable — it should suggest what to try next
or why further trials along this line are unlikely to succeed.

## Conventions

- **Λ**: The Leech lattice, as constructed in `leech_wilson.py` using Wilson's
  characterisation [Wilson2009].
- **Minimal shell**: The 196,560 vectors of squared norm 8 in Λ. These are
  generated by `leech_minimal_vectors()` in `leech_wilson.py`.
- **Wilson's membership test**: `is_in_leech(x, y, z)` from `leech_wilson.py`.
  A 24-vector is split into three octonion components (indices 0–7, 8–15, 16–23)
  and tested against Wilson's three conditions.
- **Standard octonion algebra**: `STANDARD_ALGEBRA` from `octonions.py`, using
  the Fano-plane rule e_a · e_{a+1} = e_{a+3} (mod 7 in {1,…,7}).
