"Could σ undo Coxeter?" answer — confident; added as new §10 of
companion.tex (before the Summary).  The argument rests on three
points, all already in the main paper as lemmas:

 (a) Coxeter's κ and our σ act at different structural levels.
     Coxeter's κ is a transposition of Fano triples, i.e.\ a
     rearrangement of the multiplication table that touches all
     products on the affected lines.  Our σ is a transposition of
     coordinate axes — a linear involution of R^8 that extends to
     the multiplication by conjugation a·_σ b = σ(σ(a)·σ(b)).
     Most coordinate transpositions are not Fano-plane
     automorphisms (example verified in passing: σ=(1 2) sends the
     Fano triple (2,3,5) to the non-triple (1,3,5) under our
     project convention), so σ cannot equal κ^{-1}: κ permutes
     lines, σ does not.

 (b) σ fixes L (Lemma A / Lemma~\ref{lem:sigma-L} of the main
     paper).  Therefore the σ-twist inherits Coxeter's E_8
     closure automatically:
         L ·_σ L = σ(σ(L)·σ(L)) = σ(L·L) ⊆ σ(L) = L.
     So regardless of which coordinate transposition σ we pick
     (so long as it preserves D_8^+, which holds for any
     permutation of the seven imaginary axes), Kirmse's 1924
     regime — which failed at the L level — cannot be reached.

 (c) σ does change the story at sub-L sublattices that it does
     not fix, notably Ls (σ(Ls) ≠ Ls).  This is precisely where
     the twist helps Wilson condition~(3) without disturbing
     conditions~(1) or~(2).  Coxeter's κ and our σ thus act on
     different strata of the same construction: κ fixes the E_8
     stratum; σ redirects the finer-grained Ls stratum that κ does
     not by itself settle.
