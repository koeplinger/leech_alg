Topic: Prior art — multiplicative structures / orders on the Leech lattice
=============================================================================

Literature search conducted: 2026-04-11
Purpose: Identify any prior work establishing or ruling out an order (or
ring, algebra, multiplicatively closed structure) on the Leech lattice,
before publishing our finding.

---

## Summary of search

**No prior work was found establishing an order on the Leech lattice
under an octonion-derived bilinear product.**

The specific construction in this project — three copies of a
transposition-twisted octonion algebra with Z₃ cross-block routing —
does not appear in the literature.

The closest related work falls into three categories:

1. **Wilson (2009)**: Characterises Λ as a sublattice of L³ using
   octonionic conditions, but does not define or test a multiplicative
   product on Λ.

2. **Dixon (2010)**: Defines an XY-product on triples of octonions and
   constructs the Leech lattice, but does not establish closure of Λ
   under that product.

3. **Baez / Egan (2014)**: Explores whether the Leech lattice can be
   made into a ring via the exceptional Jordan algebra (27-dimensional,
   commutative).  Finds that an integral octonionic sublattice of 𝔥₃(O)
   is closed under the doubled Jordan product 2(xy + yx), but this
   involves a factor-of-2 scaling and the Jordan (commutative) product,
   not an octonion-derived (non-commutative, non-associative) product.
   The question of closure under the standard Jordan product remains
   open.  The human researcher of this project (Koeplinger) participated
   in that discussion.

## Detailed findings

### [Wilson2009] — Octonions and the Leech lattice

Wilson constructs Λ as {(x,y,z) ∈ L³ : conditions 1–3} and describes
the Conway group Co₀ using 3×3 octonionic matrices.  The paper does not
define or test a bilinear product on Λ; it uses the octonionic structure
for characterisation and group theory, not for defining an algebra on Λ.

### [Dixon2010] — Integral Octonions, Octonion XY-Product, and the Leech Lattice

Dixon constructs the Leech lattice using the XY-product
A ∘ B = (A·l₀)(l₀⁻¹·B).  The XY-product is a different octonion
algebra on R⁸ (isomorphic to the standard one).  Dixon does not test or
claim closure of Λ under the XY-product or any other bilinear product.

### [Baez2014] — Integral Octonions, Parts 9–10 (n-Category Café)

John Baez's blog series (November–December 2014) explores the exceptional
Jordan algebra 𝔥₃(O) as a framework for the Leech lattice.  The Leech
lattice sits in the off-diagonal part of 𝔥₃(O) (24-dimensional
subspace).  Key findings:

- The lattice Z³ ⊕ L_L ⊂ 𝔥₃(O) is NOT closed under the Jordan product
  x ∘ y = xy + yx.
- It IS closed under the doubled Jordan product 2(xy + yx).
- Baez poses the open question: "A nice question is whether we can find
  a lattice like this that is closed under the Jordan product x∘y=xy+yx."
  He calls this a potential "exceptional Leech–Jordan ring."
- Greg Egan finds 17,280 sublattices that yield formally real Jordan
  rings under the doubled product.
- Baez expresses dissatisfaction with the factor of 2: "the factor of 2
  in condition c) really annoys me."
- **The human researcher (Koeplinger) is quoted in the discussion**:
  "I ran several kinds of (exceptional) Jordan products through my little
  program...but essentially didn't find anything that would close on the
  Leech inner shell."

This is the closest prior art to our finding, but it uses a fundamentally
different algebraic framework (commutative Jordan product in 27
dimensions) rather than a non-commutative, non-associative octonion-
derived product in 24 dimensions.

### [Dixon1995] — Octonions: Invariant representation of the Leech lattice

Earlier work by Dixon (1995, hep-th/9504040) on invariant octonionic
representations.  Primarily concerned with representation, not with
multiplicative closure.

### [WilsonConway] — Conway's group and octonions

Wilson describes the double cover of Conway's group Co₀ using right
multiplications by 3×3 octonionic matrices.  This uses the octonion
product to describe symmetries, not to define a closed algebra on Λ.
